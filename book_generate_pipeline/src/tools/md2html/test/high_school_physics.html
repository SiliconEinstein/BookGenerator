Problem 1

uncertainty analysis and error propagation (0)

While testing a scooter’s fuel efficiency on a weekend errand, you record the travel distance with a GPS app (distance $d$ with uncertainty $\sigma_d$) and the refueled gasoline volume using a measuring cup (volume $V$ with uncertainty $\sigma_V$); assuming independent errors, derive an expression for the propagated relative uncertainty of the efficiency $E=d/V$ in terms of $\sigma_d$, $\sigma_V$, $d$, and $V$.

Problem

On a weekend errand, you estimate your scooter’s fuel efficiency by dividing the trip distance by the gasoline volume you refuel. The trip distance is obtained from a Global Positioning System (GPS) app as a single summary value $d$ with a quoted standard uncertainty $\sigma_{d}$. The refueled gasoline volume is measured with a graduated measuring cup, giving a single reading $V$ with a standard uncertainty $\sigma_{V}$. Assume the distance and volume measurement errors are random, small compared to their respective measured values, and statistically independent.

Define the efficiency as $E=d/V$. Derive, from first principles, an analytic expression for the propagated relative standard uncertainty of $E$, that is $\sigma_{E}/E$, in terms of $d$, $V$, $\sigma_{d}$, and $\sigma_{V}$. Provide only the analytic expression as your final answer.

Solution

We are asked to determine the relative standard uncertainty of a quantity defined as a function of two independently measured quantities with small random uncertainties. The efficiency is defined as $E(d,V)=d/V$. When uncertainties are small, we can model how uncertainties propagate via a first-order (linear) approximation of the function around the measured values. For a function $E(d,V)$ with independent uncertainties in $d$ and $V$, the variance of $E$ to first order is given by the sum of the contributions from each variable: $$ \sigma_{E}^{2}=\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial d}\right)^{2}\sigma_{d}^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial V}\right)^{2}\sigma_{V}^{2}. $$ This formula follows from a first-order Taylor expansion of $E$ about $(d,V)$, $E(d+\delta d,V+\delta V)\approx E(d,V)+\frac{\partial E}{\partial d}\delta d+\frac{\partial E}{\partial V}\delta V$, and taking expectations with independent zero-mean fluctuations $\delta d$ and $\delta V$ of variances $\sigma_{d}^{2}$ and $\sigma_{V}^{2}$, respectively.

We compute the partial derivatives of $E(d,V)=d/V$: $$ \frac{\partial E}{\partial d}=\frac{1}{V},\qquad \frac{\partial E}{\partial V}=-\frac{d}{V^{2}}. $$ Substituting these into the propagation formula yields $$ \sigma_{E}^{2}=\left(\frac{1}{V}\right)^{2}\sigma_{d}^{2}+\left(-\frac{d}{V^{2}}\right)^{2}\sigma_{V}^{2}=\frac{\sigma_{d}^{2}}{V^{2}}+\frac{d^{2}}{V^{4}}\sigma_{V}^{2}. $$ The relative standard uncertainty is the standard uncertainty divided by the magnitude of the quantity itself. Since $E=d/V$, we have $E^{2}=d^{2}/V^{2}$. Dividing $\sigma_{E}^{2}$ by $E^{2}$ gives $$ \left(\frac{\sigma_{E}}{E}\right)^{2}=\frac{\sigma_{E}^{2}}{E^{2}}=\frac{\frac{\sigma_{d}^{2}}{V^{2}}+\frac{d^{2}}{V^{4}}\sigma_{V}^{2}}{\frac{d^{2}}{V^{2}}}=\frac{\sigma_{d}^{2}}{d^{2}}+\frac{\sigma_{V}^{2}}{V^{2}}. $$ Taking the positive square root (standard deviations are nonnegative) yields the propagated relative standard uncertainty: $$ \frac{\sigma_{E}}{E}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{d}}{d}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\sigma_{V}}{V}\right)^{2}}. $$ This expression depends only on the relative uncertainties of $d$ and $V$ and holds under the assumptions of small, independent uncertainties and first-order propagation.

Answer

$$\boxed{\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{d}}{d}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\sigma_{V}}{V}\right)^{2}}}$$

Problem 2

inclined plane motion with friction (0)

A warehouse worker uses a spring scale inline with a loading ramp to pull a flat-bottomed crate of mass $m$ up at constant speed; derive the required scale reading $F(\theta)$ as a function of ramp angle $\theta$, gravity $g$, and kinetic friction coefficient $\mu_k$ between crate and ramp.

Problem

A warehouse installs a quality-control fixture on its adjustable loading ramp: a spring scale is built inline with a tow strap that runs parallel to the ramp surface. A flat-bottomed crate of mass $m$ is pulled by the strap up the straight ramp at constant speed. The kinetic friction coefficient between the crate and the ramp surface is uniform and equal to $\mu_k$. The ramp makes an angle $\theta$ with the horizontal, and $\theta$ is measured in radians. Gravitational acceleration has magnitude $g$. Air resistance and any pulley mass or strap sag can be neglected, and the strap force is exactly parallel to the ramp surface.

Derive an analytic expression $F(\theta)$ for the scale’s reading as a function of $\theta$, $m$, $g$, and $\mu_k$. Provide the expression only; do not include units in the final answer.

Solution

We model the crate as a particle and choose axes aligned with the ramp: one axis along the ramp (positive upslope) and one perpendicular to the ramp surface (positive outward from the surface). The forces on the crate are:

  • Weight $\vec{W}$ of magnitude $mg$ acting vertically downward.
  • Normal force $\vec{N}$ from the ramp acting perpendicular to the surface.
  • Kinetic friction $\vec{f}_k$ acting along the ramp opposite the direction of motion; since the crate moves upslope, friction acts downslope with magnitude $f_k=\mu_k N$.
  • Tension (the scale reading) $\vec{F}$ acting up the ramp, parallel to the surface.

Resolve the weight into components relative to the ramp:

  • Along the ramp (downslope component): $mg\sin(\theta)$.
  • Perpendicular to the ramp: $mg\cos(\theta)$ pressing the crate into the surface.

Because the strap force is parallel to the surface, it has no perpendicular component. With the crate moving at constant speed, the acceleration is zero, so the net force vanishes in both directions.

Perpendicular to the ramp, Newton’s second law gives: $$N - mg\cos(\theta) = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad N = mg\cos(\theta).$$
