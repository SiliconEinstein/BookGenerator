## Problem 1

**Topic**: uncertainty analysis and error propagation (0)

**Thumbnail**: While testing a scooter’s fuel efficiency on a weekend errand, you record the travel distance with a GPS app (distance $d$ with uncertainty $\sigma_d$) and the refueled gasoline volume using a measuring cup (volume $V$ with uncertainty $\sigma_V$); assuming independent errors, derive an expression for the propagated relative uncertainty of the efficiency $E=d/V$ in terms of $\sigma_d$, $\sigma_V$, $d$, and $V$.

### Problem

On a weekend errand, you estimate your scooter’s fuel efficiency by dividing the trip distance by the gasoline volume you refuel. The trip distance is obtained from a Global Positioning System (GPS) app as a single summary value $d$ with a quoted standard uncertainty $\sigma_{d}$. The refueled gasoline volume is measured with a graduated measuring cup, giving a single reading $V$ with a standard uncertainty $\sigma_{V}$. Assume the distance and volume measurement errors are random, small compared to their respective measured values, and statistically independent.

Define the efficiency as $E=d/V$. Derive, from first principles, an analytic expression for the propagated relative standard uncertainty of $E$, that is $\sigma_{E}/E$, in terms of $d$, $V$, $\sigma_{d}$, and $\sigma_{V}$. Provide only the analytic expression as your final answer.

### Solution

We are asked to determine the relative standard uncertainty of a quantity defined as a function of two independently measured quantities with small random uncertainties. The efficiency is defined as $E(d,V)=d/V$. When uncertainties are small, we can model how uncertainties propagate via a first-order (linear) approximation of the function around the measured values. For a function $E(d,V)$ with independent uncertainties in $d$ and $V$, the variance of $E$ to first order is given by the sum of the contributions from each variable:
$$
\sigma_{E}^{2}=\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial d}\right)^{2}\sigma_{d}^{2}+\left(\frac{\partial E}{\partial V}\right)^{2}\sigma_{V}^{2}.
$$
This formula follows from a first-order Taylor expansion of $E$ about $(d,V)$, $E(d+\delta d,V+\delta V)\approx E(d,V)+\frac{\partial E}{\partial d}\delta d+\frac{\partial E}{\partial V}\delta V$, and taking expectations with independent zero-mean fluctuations $\delta d$ and $\delta V$ of variances $\sigma_{d}^{2}$ and $\sigma_{V}^{2}$, respectively.

We compute the partial derivatives of $E(d,V)=d/V$:
$$
\frac{\partial E}{\partial d}=\frac{1}{V},\qquad \frac{\partial E}{\partial V}=-\frac{d}{V^{2}}.
$$
Substituting these into the propagation formula yields
$$
\sigma_{E}^{2}=\left(\frac{1}{V}\right)^{2}\sigma_{d}^{2}+\left(-\frac{d}{V^{2}}\right)^{2}\sigma_{V}^{2}=\frac{\sigma_{d}^{2}}{V^{2}}+\frac{d^{2}}{V^{4}}\sigma_{V}^{2}.
$$
The relative standard uncertainty is the standard uncertainty divided by the magnitude of the quantity itself. Since $E=d/V$, we have $E^{2}=d^{2}/V^{2}$. Dividing $\sigma_{E}^{2}$ by $E^{2}$ gives
$$
\left(\frac{\sigma_{E}}{E}\right)^{2}=\frac{\sigma_{E}^{2}}{E^{2}}=\frac{\frac{\sigma_{d}^{2}}{V^{2}}+\frac{d^{2}}{V^{4}}\sigma_{V}^{2}}{\frac{d^{2}}{V^{2}}}=\frac{\sigma_{d}^{2}}{d^{2}}+\frac{\sigma_{V}^{2}}{V^{2}}.
$$
Taking the positive square root (standard deviations are nonnegative) yields the propagated relative standard uncertainty:
$$
\frac{\sigma_{E}}{E}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{d}}{d}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\sigma_{V}}{V}\right)^{2}}.
$$
This expression depends only on the relative uncertainties of $d$ and $V$ and holds under the assumptions of small, independent uncertainties and first-order propagation.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_{d}}{d}\right)^{2}+\left(\frac{\sigma_{V}}{V}\right)^{2}}}$$

### Solution

We model the crate as a particle and choose axes aligned with the ramp: one axis along the ramp (positive upslope) and one perpendicular to the ramp surface (positive outward from the surface). The forces on the crate are:
- Weight $\vec{W}$ of magnitude $mg$ acting vertically downward.
- Normal force $\vec{N}$ from the ramp acting perpendicular to the surface.
- Kinetic friction $\vec{f}_k$ acting along the ramp opposite the direction of motion; since the crate moves upslope, friction acts downslope with magnitude $f_k=\mu_k N$.
- Tension (the scale reading) $\vec{F}$ acting up the ramp, parallel to the surface.

Resolve the weight into components relative to the ramp:
- Along the ramp (downslope component): $mg\sin(\theta)$.
- Perpendicular to the ramp: $mg\cos(\theta)$ pressing the crate into the surface.

$$F - mg\sin(\theta) - \mu_k\big(mg\cos(\theta)\big) = 0.$$

**Topic**: wave interference and superposition (0)

**Thumbnail**: Along a sidewalk parallel to a reflective glass wall, derive the formula for the nearest walking position where a listener at ear height $h$ hears a minimum from a portable speaker at the same height a horizontal distance $x$ from the wall, using the image-source idea and the condition for destructive interference in terms of $x$, $h$, $v$, and $f$.

$$
L_{d}=\sqrt{(x-x)^{2}+(s-0)^{2}}=s.
$$
The image of the source across the wall lies at $(-x,0)$. The reflected path length equals the distance from this image to the listener:
$$
L_{r}=\sqrt{(x-(-x))^{2}+(s-0)^{2}}=\sqrt{(2x)^{2}+s^{2}}.
$$
With no phase flip upon reflection, the phase difference at the listener is set solely by the path difference $\Delta=L_{r}-L_{d}$. For a tone of wavelength $\lambda=\frac{v}{f}$, destructive interference (a local minimum) occurs when the path difference equals an odd multiple of half-wavelength:
$$
\Delta=L_{r}-L_{d}=\left(m+\frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda,\quad m=0,1,2,\dots
$$
Substitute the expressions for $L_{r}$ and $L_{d}$:
$$
\sqrt{4x^{2}+s^{2}}-s=\left(m+\frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda.
$$

$$
\sqrt{4x^{2}+s^{2}}=s+\left(m+\frac{1}{2}\right)\lambda.
$$

$$

$$

$$
4x^{2}=s\left(2m+1\right)\lambda+\left(m+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}\lambda^{2},
$$
$$
s=\frac{4x^{2}-\left(m+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}\lambda^{2}}{\left(2m+1\right)\lambda}.

This expression is in meters because $x$ is a length and $v/f$ has units of length.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{4x^{2}f}{v}-\frac{v}{4f}}$$

**Topic**: conservation of mechanical energy (0)

**Thumbnail**: A teenager of mass $m$ steps off a platform a vertical distance $H$ above a backyard trampoline modeled as a horizontal spring with effective constant $k$ (neglecting air resistance and losses), and using conservation of mechanical energy derive an explicit expression for the trampoline’s maximum depression $x_{\max}$ in terms of $m$, $g$, $H$, and $k$.

A backyard trampoline is engineered to behave like a single effective vertical spring with stiffness constant $k$. Derive an explicit expression for $x_{\max}$ in terms of $m$, $g$, $H$, and $k$.

### Solution

We model the trampoline as a vertical spring with stiffness $k$, attached to the ground, with its undeformed surface at height taken as a reference level. The teenager begins at rest on a platform at height $H$ above this reference level, then falls, contacts the trampoline at the reference level (undeformed), and continues moving downward as the trampoline compresses. The maximum depression $x_{\max}$ occurs at the instant when the teenager’s downward speed becomes zero for the first time during the compression.

We assume mechanical energy is conserved because air resistance and internal losses are neglected. Choose the zero of gravitational potential energy at the undeformed trampoline surface. Let downward displacement be denoted by $x$ from that surface. At the initial instant (just before stepping off), the teenager is at height $H$ above the reference, at rest, and the spring is uncompressed. The initial total mechanical energy $E_{\text{i}}$ is purely gravitational potential energy:
$$
E_{\text{i}} = m g H + 0 + 0,
$$
where the terms correspond to gravitational potential energy of the teenager, spring potential energy, and kinetic energy, respectively.

At the instant of maximum compression $x_{\max}$, the teenager is momentarily at rest, the spring is compressed by $x_{\max}$, and the teenager has descended a total distance $H + x_{\max}$ from the initial position. The total mechanical energy $E_{\text{f}}$ at that instant consists of the spring potential energy plus the gravitational potential energy relative to the reference:
$$
E_{\text{f}} = \frac{1}{2} k x_{\max}^{2} + m g (0 - (H + x_{\max})) + 0.
$$
However, since we defined zero gravitational potential energy at the reference level, it is simpler to account for energy changes by equating the initial energy at height $H$ to the energy stored in the spring plus the decrease in gravitational potential energy. An equivalent and clearer way is to write energy conservation between the initial state and the state at maximum compression as:
$$
m g H = \frac{1}{2} k x_{\max}^{2} - m g x_{\max}.
$$
This equation states that the initial gravitational potential energy $m g H$ is converted into the spring potential energy at compression minus the reduction in gravitational potential energy from the reference level down to $-x_{\max}$, which is $-m g x_{\max}$ relative to the reference. Rearranging all terms to one side gives a standard quadratic equation in $x_{\max}$:
$$
\frac{1}{2} k x_{\max}^{2} - m g x_{\max} - m g H = 0.
$$
Multiply both sides by $2$ to simplify coefficients:
$$
k x_{\max}^{2} - 2 m g x_{\max} - 2 m g H = 0.
$$
This is a quadratic equation of the form $a x^{2} + b x + c = 0$ with $a = k$, $b = -2 m g$, and $c = -2 m g H$. Solving for $x_{\max}$ using the quadratic formula yields:
$$
x_{\max} = \frac{2 m g \pm \sqrt{(2 m g)^{2} - 4 k (-2 m g H)}}{2 k}
= \frac{2 m g \pm \sqrt{4 m^{2} g^{2} + 8 k m g H}}{2 k}.
$$
Factor $4$ from the square root:
$$
x_{\max} = \frac{2 m g \pm 2 \sqrt{m^{2} g^{2} + 2 k m g H}}{2 k}
= \frac{m g \pm \sqrt{m^{2} g^{2} + 2 k m g H}}{k}.
$$
We must select the physically meaningful root. Since $\sqrt{m^{2} g^{2} + 2 k m g H} > m g$ for any $H > 0$, the root with the minus sign would yield a negative value of $x_{\max}$, which is unphysical for a downward compression. Therefore, we take the plus sign:
$$
x_{\max} = \frac{m g + \sqrt{m^{2} g^{2} + 2 k m g H}}{k}.
$$
This expression has dimensions of length, as required, and reduces to $x_{\max} = \frac{2 m g}{k}$ when $H = 0$, consistent with a step from the undeformed surface level.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{m g + \sqrt{m^{2} g^{2} + 2 k m g H}}{k}}$$

Ignoring the trivial root $t=0$, the flight time to the slope is
$$
t=\frac{2v\left(\sin\theta+\cos\theta\,\tan\alpha\right)}{g}.
$$
Using the identity $\sin\theta+\cos\theta\,\tan\alpha=\dfrac{\sin(\theta+\alpha)}{\cos\alpha}$, this simplifies to
$$
t=\frac{2v}{g}\cdot\frac{\sin(\theta+\alpha)}{\cos\alpha}.
$$
The horizontal displacement at landing is then
$$
x=v\cos\theta\,t=\frac{2v^{2}\cos\theta\,\sin(\theta+\alpha)}{g\cos\alpha}.
$$
The distance measured along the slope from the origin to the landing point is the length of the projection of this displacement onto the slope line. For a line making angle $-\alpha$ with the horizontal, the along-slope distance $S$ relates to the horizontal displacement by
$$
S=\frac{x}{\cos\alpha}.
$$
Therefore,
$$
S=\frac{2v^{2}\cos\theta\,\sin(\theta+\alpha)}{g\cos^{2}\alpha}.
$$
Recall that $\theta=\beta-\alpha$, so $\sin(\theta+\alpha)=\sin\beta$. Hence
$$
S(\beta)=\frac{2v^{2}}{g\cos^{2}\alpha}\,\cos(\beta-\alpha)\,\sin\beta.
$$
Since the prefactor $\dfrac{2v^{2}}{g\cos^{2}\alpha}$ is positive and independent of $\beta$, maximizing $S$ over $\beta$ is equivalent to maximizing
$$
f(\beta)=\cos(\beta-\alpha)\,\sin\beta.
$$
Differentiate with respect to $\beta$:
$$
f'(\beta)=-\sin(\beta-\alpha)\,\sin\beta+\cos(\beta-\alpha)\,\cos\beta.
$$
Using the cosine addition identity, this simplifies to
$$
f'(\beta)=\cos\big((\beta-\alpha)+\beta\big)=\cos(2\beta-\alpha).
$$
Critical points satisfy $f'(\beta)=0$, i.e.,
$$
\cos(2\beta-\alpha)=0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad 2\beta-\alpha=\frac{\pi}{2}+k\pi,\quad k\in\mathbb{Z}.
$$
To verify which critical point gives a maximum, compute the second derivative:
$$
f''(\beta)=-2\sin(2\beta-\alpha).
$$
At $2\beta-\alpha=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$ (i.e., $k=0$), we have $f''(\beta)=-2\sin\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)=-2<0$, confirming a local maximum. Other solutions with $k\neq 0$ either correspond to minima or angles outside the physically relevant range for “above the slope.” Therefore, the maximizing angle is
$$
\beta=\frac{\pi}{4}+\frac{\alpha}{2}.
$$
This result is independent of $v$ and $g$, as expected for an optimization of range angle under uniform gravity without air resistance, and it is already expressed in radians in terms of $\alpha$ only.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{\pi}{4}+\frac{\alpha}{2}}$$

## Problem 11

**Topic**: energy in pendulum motion (0)

**Thumbnail**: In a home cold-brew setup, a metal ball on a string swings through a thin honey stream exactly at the lowest point each pass and loses a fixed fraction $k$ of its kinetic energy there; starting from release angle $\theta_0$ on a string of length $L$, derive an expression for the maximum angle $\theta_n$ after the $n$-th swing using energy relations (you may use $h \approx \tfrac{1}{2}L\theta^2$ for small angles).

### Problem

A home cold-brew jar is fitted with a light, inextensible string of length $L$ holding a small solid metal ball of mass $m$ as a pendulum. A very thin vertical stream of honey crosses exactly through the pendulum’s lowest point. Each time the ball passes through the lowest point, it experiences a brief interaction with the honey that removes a fixed fraction $k$ (where $0<k<1$) of the ball’s kinetic energy at that instant. All other sources of friction and air resistance are negligible. The ball is released from rest from an initial angular displacement $\theta_{0}$ (measured from the downward vertical), and subsequent swings remain in the small-angle regime. You may approximate the vertical height of the ball above the lowest point at angular displacement $\theta$ by $h \approx \tfrac{1}{2}L\theta^{2}$. Use $g$ for the gravitational acceleration. All angles are to be expressed in radians.

Let $\theta_{n}$ denote the maximum angular displacement reached after the $n$-th pass through the honey stream (i.e., after $n$ crossings of the lowest point). Derive a closed-form expression for $\theta_{n}$ in terms of $n$, $k$, $\theta_{0}$, and any other given parameters. Express $\theta_{n}$ in radians and provide only the analytic expression in the final answer.

### Solution

We model the pendulum’s energy changes between turning points and the lowest point. For small angles, the vertical height above the lowest point at angle $\theta$ is $h \approx \tfrac{1}{2}L\theta^{2}$. The gravitational potential energy relative to the lowest point is therefore
$$
U(\theta) \approx mgh \approx \tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta^{2}.
$$
When the ball is released from rest at angle $\theta_{0}$, its potential energy relative to the lowest point is $U(\theta_{0}) \approx \tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{0}^{2}$. Neglecting losses until the lowest point, this converts to kinetic energy just before the first crossing of the lowest point:
$$
K_{\text{before},1} = \tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{0}^{2}.
$$
At the lowest point, the honey removes a fixed fraction $k$ of the kinetic energy. Thus the kinetic energy immediately after crossing is
$$
K_{\text{after},1} = (1-k)K_{\text{before},1} = (1-k)\tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{0}^{2}.
$$
The ball then rises to a new turning point at which the kinetic energy is zero and all of $K_{\text{after},1}$ has been converted back into potential energy at angle $\theta_{1}$:
$$
\tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{1}^{2} = (1-k)\tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{0}^{2}.
$$
Canceling common factors gives
$$
\theta_{1}^{2} = (1-k)\theta_{0}^{2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta_{1} = \theta_{0}\sqrt{1-k},
$$
taking the positive root for the magnitude of the turning angle.

This process repeats identically on each subsequent pass through the lowest point: just before the $(n+1)$-th crossing of the lowest point, the kinetic energy equals the potential energy at the previous turning point, $\tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{n}^{2}$. Immediately after crossing, the kinetic energy becomes $(1-k)\tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{n}^{2}$, which then converts to potential energy at the next turning point $\theta_{n+1}$:
$$
\tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{n+1}^{2} = (1-k)\tfrac{1}{2}mgL\theta_{n}^{2}.
$$
Again canceling common factors yields the recurrence
$$
\theta_{n+1}^{2} = (1-k)\theta_{n}^{2}.
$$
Applying this $n$ times starting from $\theta_{0}$ gives
$$
\theta_{n}^{2} = (1-k)^{n}\theta_{0}^{2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta_{n} = \theta_{0}(1-k)^{n/2}.
$$
This expression is in radians and depends only on the given parameters, with no additional constants remaining.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\theta_{0}(1-k)^{n/2}}$$

## Problem 14

**Topic**: linear momentum and impulse calculations (0)

**Thumbnail**: In a parcel-sorting line, a rubber-coated pneumatic pusher accelerates a stationary box of mass $m$ along a side chute by exerting a triangular force pulse that rises linearly from $0$ to $F_{\max}$ in time $\tau/2$ and falls back to $0$ in the next $\tau/2$; derive an expression for $F_{\max}$ needed to deliver the box an exit speed $v$ using impulse-momentum.

### Problem

On a parcel-sorting line, a rubber-coated pneumatic pusher contacts a stationary box of mass $m$ on a level, low-friction chute and drives it along a straight horizontal path. During the contact interval $0 \leq t \leq \tau$, the horizontal force the pusher exerts on the box varies in time according to the piecewise-linear profile
- $F(t)=\dfrac{2F_{\max}}{\tau}\,t$ for $0 \leq t \leq \dfrac{\tau}{2}$,
- $F(t)=\dfrac{2F_{\max}}{\tau}\,(\tau - t)$ for $\dfrac{\tau}{2} \leq t \leq \tau$,
and $F(t)=0$ outside this interval. The box does not slip relative to the pusher during contact, and all other horizontal forces (such as kinetic friction with the chute and air drag) are negligible. The box starts from rest at $t=0$.

Determine the expression for the peak force $F_{\max}$ required so that the box has speed $v$ exactly at $t=\tau$ when the pusher’s contact ends. Express your answer as a simplified analytic expression in terms of $m$, $v$, and $\tau$ only.

### Solution

We model the motion along the horizontal direction. The normal force and the box’s weight act vertically and cancel, and all other horizontal forces are neglected. Therefore, the net horizontal force on the box during contact is the pusher force $F(t)$ given.

The relevant principle is the impulse–momentum theorem, which relates the change in linear momentum to the time integral of the net force:
$$
\Delta p = m v_{\text{final}} - m v_{\text{initial}} = \int_{0}^{\tau} F(t)\,dt.
$$
The box starts from rest, so $v_{\text{initial}}=0$, and we require $v_{\text{final}}=v$ at $t=\tau$. Thus
$$
m v = \int_{0}^{\tau} F(t)\,dt.
$$
Using the given piecewise definition of $F(t)$, we split the integral at $t=\tau/2$:
$$
\int_{0}^{\tau} F(t)\,dt
= \int_{0}^{\tau/2} \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} t \, dt \;+\; \int_{\tau/2}^{\tau} \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} (\tau - t) \, dt.
$$
Compute the first integral:
$$
\int_{0}^{\tau/2} \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} t \, dt
= \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} \left[ \frac{t^{2}}{2} \right]_{0}^{\tau/2}
= \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} \cdot \frac{(\tau/2)^{2}}{2}
= \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} \cdot \frac{\tau^{2}}{8}
= \frac{F_{\max}\,\tau}{4}.
$$
Compute the second integral:
$$
\int_{\tau/2}^{\tau} \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} (\tau - t) \, dt
= \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} \int_{\tau/2}^{\tau} (\tau - t) \, dt
= \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} \left[ \tau t - \frac{t^{2}}{2} \right]_{\tau/2}^{\tau}.
$$
Evaluate the bracket:
$$
\left[ \tau t - \frac{t^{2}}{2} \right]_{\tau/2}^{\tau}
= \left( \tau^{2} - \frac{\tau^{2}}{2} \right) - \left( \tau \cdot \frac{\tau}{2} - \frac{(\tau/2)^{2}}{2} \right)
= \left( \frac{\tau^{2}}{2} \right) - \left( \frac{\tau^{2}}{2} - \frac{\tau^{2}}{8} \right)
= \frac{\tau^{2}}{8}.
$$
Thus,
$$
\int_{\tau/2}^{\tau} \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} (\tau - t) \, dt
= \frac{2F_{\max}}{\tau} \cdot \frac{\tau^{2}}{8}
= \frac{F_{\max}\,\tau}{4}.
$$
Adding both contributions gives the total impulse:
$$
\int_{0}^{\tau} F(t)\,dt = \frac{F_{\max}\,\tau}{4} + \frac{F_{\max}\,\tau}{4} = \frac{F_{\max}\,\tau}{2}.
$$
Equating this to $m v$ and solving for $F_{\max}$:
$$
m v = \frac{F_{\max}\,\tau}{2}
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
F_{\max} = \frac{2 m v}{\tau}.
$$
This is the required peak force in terms of $m$, $v$, and $\tau$.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{2 m v}{\tau}}$$

**Topic**: electric charge quantization and conservation (0)

A high-impedance voltmeter measures the car body’s electric potential relative to ground; the meter’s input draws negligible charge on the time scale of the events described, and its own input capacitance can be neglected compared to $C$. A nearby air ionizer occasionally sends a single free electron onto the car’s body. Each such arrival is extremely brief compared to any slow leakage of charge to the environment, and the time between arrivals is long enough that the meter reading settles between events.

Let $e$ denote the magnitude of the elementary charge carried by one electron. Starting from an arbitrary initial potential, derive an analytic expression, in terms of $e$ and $C$ only, for the magnitude of the voltage step that the voltmeter registers at the instant a single electron lands on the car. Then justify, within this model, why the meter’s stepwise changes cannot be non-integer multiples of this value. Provide your final answer as a symbolic expression in terms of $e$ and $C$ only; no numerical evaluation is required.

### Solution

We model the car as a single conductor with capacitance $C$ to ground. By the definition of capacitance, the potential of the conductor relative to ground is related to its net charge by
$$
V=\frac{Q}{C}.
$$
Consider the state immediately before an electron arrives: the car has some charge $Q_{i}$ and potential $V_{i}=Q_{i}/C$. When one electron lands on the car, the car’s net charge changes by the electron’s charge. Since the electron carries charge $-e$ (with $e>0$ denoting the magnitude), the car’s charge changes by $\Delta Q=-e$, so the new charge is
$$
Q_{f}=Q_{i}+\Delta Q=Q_{i}-e.
$$
The corresponding new potential is
$$
V_{f}=\frac{Q_{f}}{C}=\frac{Q_{i}-e}{C}=V_{i}-\frac{e}{C}.
$$
Therefore, the instantaneous change in potential caused by the arrival of a single electron is
$$
\Delta V=V_{f}-V_{i}=-\frac{e}{C}.
$$
The magnitude of the voltage step that the meter registers is thus
$$
\left|\Delta V\right|=\frac{e}{C}.
$$

To explain why step sizes cannot be non-integer multiples of this value within this model, we invoke two facts about charge on the car during these events:

1) Charge conservation at the instant of an event: over the very short time during which an electron arrives, there is no other significant path for charge to enter or leave the car (the voltmeter’s input draws negligible charge and we neglect any slow leakage over this instant). Thus the car’s net charge can change only by the charge carried by the arriving particle(s).

2) Charge quantization: the charge carriers in this process are electrons, each carrying charge $-e$. Consequently, each discrete arrival changes the car’s charge by an integer multiple of $e$; one electron changes it by $-e$, two by $-2e$, and so on. After $n$ such arrivals, with $n$ a nonnegative integer, the total change in the car’s charge is $\Delta Q=-ne$, and the total change in potential is
$$
\Delta V_{\text{total}}=\frac{\Delta Q}{C}=-\frac{ne}{C}.
$$
Hence the meter’s stepwise changes must be integer multiples of $e/C$ in magnitude, namely $n(e/C)$, with $n\in\{1,2,3,\dots\}$. Any non-integer multiple would correspond to a non-integer multiple of $e$ being transferred to the car in a single event, which is not allowed when the only charge carriers are electrons and the meter does not source or sink fractional charge. Therefore, within the stated model, step sizes cannot be non-integer multiples of $e/C$.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{e}{C}}$$

**Topic**: energy method problem solving (0)

**Thumbnail**: A smartphone dolly on a kitchen-counter slider is pulled back by a rubber exercise band of spring constant $k$ and released to roll a distance $D$ across the counter (kinetic friction coefficient $\mu_k$) before climbing a small ramp of angle $\theta$ to just reach a platform of height $h$; derive an expression for the minimum initial stretch $x$ required in terms of $m, k, \mu_k, D, \theta, h$, and $g$ using energy considerations.

The band is anchored at the release point and ceases to exert any force once it has returned to its relaxed length, after which it is slack.

From the starting point, the dolly travels a horizontal distance $D$ across the counter, then immediately climbs a straight ramp that makes a constant angle $\theta$ (measured in radians) with the horizontal, leading to a platform whose top surface is a vertical height $h$ above the counter. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the dolly and every surface it contacts (the horizontal counter and the ramp) is the same constant $\mu_k$. Air resistance is negligible. ### Solution

Take gravitational potential energy to be zero at the height of the counter. The spring is anchored at the start location and goes slack once it returns to its relaxed length, so its potential energy is zero during the subsequent motion.

We apply the work-energy principle including potential energies and non-conservative work. Let the final state be the moment the dolly just arrives at the platform height $h$ with zero speed. The final mechanical energy is then purely gravitational potential energy $m g h$. The only non-conservative force doing work is kinetic friction along the path on the horizontal counter and up the ramp.

On the horizontal segment of length $D$, the normal force is $N = m g$, so the friction magnitude is $\mu_k m g$, and the work done by friction is negative:
$$
W_{\text{fr},\text{flat}} = -\mu_k m g D.
$$

On the inclined ramp, let the ramp length required to gain height $h$ be $s$. With ramp angle $\theta$, the length is $s = \frac{h}{\sin(\theta)}$. The normal force on the ramp is $N = m g \cos(\theta)$, so the friction magnitude is $\mu_k m g \cos(\theta)$, directed opposite the motion. The work done by friction on the ramp is then
$$
W_{\text{fr},\text{ramp}} = -\mu_k m g \cos(\theta)\, s = -\mu_k m g \cos(\theta)\,\frac{h}{\sin(\theta)} = -\mu_k m g h \cot(\theta).
$$

Summing the non-conservative work over both segments gives
$$
W_{\text{fr, total}} = -\mu_k m g D - \mu_k m g h \cot(\theta).
$$

Using the energy balance from the initial moment of release to the final arrival at height $h$:
$$
\text{Initial energy} + \text{Work by non-conservative forces} = \text{Final energy}.
$$

### Solution

We model the configuration as two homogeneous layers stacked along the field direction: a plastic layer of thickness $s\cos(\theta)$ with permittivity $\varepsilon_{1}=k\varepsilon_{0}$, and an air layer of thickness $d-s\cos(\theta)$ with permittivity $\varepsilon_{2}=\varepsilon_{0}$. $$
E_{1}=\frac{D}{\varepsilon_{1}}=\frac{Q}{A\,k\varepsilon_{0}},\qquad
E_{2}=\frac{D}{\varepsilon_{2}}=\frac{Q}{A\,\varepsilon_{0}}.
$$
The total potential difference is the sum of drops across the two layers:
$$
V=E_{1}\bigl(s\cos(\theta)\bigr)+E_{2}\bigl(d-s\cos(\theta)\bigr)
=\frac{Q}{A}\left[\frac{s\cos(\theta)}{k\varepsilon_{0}}+\frac{d-s\cos(\theta)}{\varepsilon_{0}}\right].
$$
The capacitance is $C(\theta)=Q/V$, hence
$$
C(\theta)=\frac{Q}{V}
=\frac{Q}{\dfrac{Q}{A}\left[\dfrac{s\cos(\theta)}{k\varepsilon_{0}}+\dfrac{d-s\cos(\theta)}{\varepsilon_{0}}\right]}
=\frac{A}{\dfrac{s\cos(\theta)}{k\varepsilon_{0}}+\dfrac{d-s\cos(\theta)}{\varepsilon_{0}}}.

$$
C(\theta)=\frac{\varepsilon_{0}A}{\left(d-s\cos(\theta)\right)+\dfrac{s\cos(\theta)}{k}}

This can be written in a slightly more compact form by combining the last two terms:

$$
This expression is in terms of $A$, $d$, $s$, $\theta$, $k$, and $\varepsilon_{0}$ as required and is valid for $0\le s\cos(\theta)\le d$.

$$\boxed{\frac{\varepsilon_{0}A}{d - s\cos(\theta)\left(1 - \frac{1}{k}\right)}}$$

### Solution

We construct the model of the gas and process. The air is modeled as an ideal gas, so at any instant its pressure $P$, volume $V$, amount of substance $n$, and temperature $T$ satisfy the ideal gas equation of state $PV=nRT$. The process is described as very slow (quasi-static), so the gas pressure equals the external pressure acting on it at each instant. It is also isothermal at temperature $T$ because the large water bath maintains constant temperature.

We define the sign convention for work as follows: the work done by the gas on the surroundings for an infinitesimal volume change $dV$ in a quasi-static process is $\delta W_{\text{by}}=P\,dV$. Therefore, the infinitesimal work done on the gas is the negative of that, $\delta W_{\text{on}}=-\delta W_{\text{by}}=-P\,dV$. For a compression where $V$ decreases, $dV<0$, so $\delta W_{\text{on}}$ is positive, as expected.

The total work done on the gas as the volume changes from $V_1$ to $V_2$ is
$$
W_{\text{on}}=\int_{V_1}^{V_2}(-P)\,dV.
$$
Using the ideal gas relation during the isothermal process, $P=\dfrac{nRT}{V}$, we substitute:
$$
W_{\text{on}}=\int_{V_1}^{V_2}\left(-\frac{nRT}{V}\right)\,dV.
$$
Since $n$, $R$, and $T$ are constants during the process, we take them outside the integral:
$$
W_{\text{on}}=-nRT\int_{V_1}^{V_2}\frac{1}{V}\,dV.
$$
Evaluating the integral,
$$
W_{\text{on}}=-nRT\left[\ln V\right]_{V_1}^{V_2}=-nRT\left(\ln V_2-\ln V_1\right).
$$
Using logarithm properties,
$$
W_{\text{on}}=nRT\ln\!\left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right).
$$
This is the desired closed-form expression in terms of $n$, $R$, $T$, $V_1$, and $V_2$ for the total mechanical work done on the gas during the isothermal compression. The expression is positive when $V_2<V_1$, consistent with work being done on the gas.

### Answer

$$\boxed{nRT\,\ln\!\left(\frac{V_{1}}{V_{2}}\right)}$$

### Solution

We model the vertical motion of the ball between impacts as uniformly accelerated motion under gravity with acceleration of magnitude $g$, directed downward. At each collision with the floor, the coefficient of restitution $e$ scales the speed instantaneously: if $v_{\text{before}}$ is the magnitude of the speed just before impact, then the magnitude just after is $v_{\text{after}} = e\,v_{\text{before}}$.

Let the magnitude of the speed just before the first impact be $v_{1}^{-}$, and just after the first impact be $v_{1}^{+}$. By the restitution definition, $v_{1}^{+} = e\,v_{1}^{-}$. Therefore, the time between the first and second impacts is
$$
\Delta t_{1} = \frac{v_{1}^{+}}{g} + \frac{v_{1}^{+}}{g} = \frac{2\,v_{1}^{+}}{g}.
$$

After the second impact, the speed becomes $v_{2}^{+} = e\,v_{2}^{-} = e\,v_{1}^{+}$. Repeating the same flight-time reasoning for the interval between the second and third impacts,
$$
\Delta t_{2} = \frac{2\,v_{2}^{+}}{g} = \frac{2\,e\,v_{1}^{+}}{g}.

$$
\Delta t_{1} = \frac{2\,v_{1}^{+}}{g}, \quad \Delta t_{2} = \frac{2\,e\,v_{1}^{+}}{g}.

$$
\frac{\Delta t_{2}}{\Delta t_{1}} = e.
$$

$$\boxed{\frac{\Delta t_{2}}{\Delta t_{1}}}$$

## Problem 24

**Topic**: beat frequency phenomena (1)

**Thumbnail**: Standing on a sidewalk below two rooftop exhaust fans with $B_1$ and $B_2$ blades rotating at $N$ rpm and $(N+\Delta N)$ rpm, respectively, you hear a slow “wah-wah” in their combined hum; model each fan’s dominant tone by its blade-pass frequency and derive the beat frequency of the loudness and the time between successive maxima in terms of $B_1,B_2,N$, and $\Delta N$.

### Problem

You are standing on a sidewalk beneath two rooftop exhaust fans mounted on the same building, far enough away that path differences and reflections can be neglected. Fan 1 has $B_{1}$ identical blades and rotates at a steady $N$ revolutions per minute (rpm). Fan 2 has $B_{2}$ identical blades and rotates at a steady $(N+\Delta N)$ rpm. Assume $B_{1}$ and $B_{2}$ are positive integers and $N>0$, while $\Delta N$ may be positive or negative but $\left| \Delta N \right| \ll N$ so that you perceive a slow variation in loudness.

Model the acoustic contribution of each fan at your location as a single pure sinusoidal tone at its blade-pass frequency, with equal amplitude and negligible phase offset at $t=0$. The blade-pass frequency of a fan is defined as the product of its number of blades and its rotational frequency in revolutions per second.

Using only these assumptions and the linear superposition of sound waves, derive:
- the beat frequency of the loudness that you perceive (in hertz), and
- the time between successive maxima in the perceived loudness (in seconds),

expressed in terms of $B_{1}$, $B_{2}$, $N$, and $\Delta N$.

Express your final result as a single row matrix $\begin{pmatrix} f_{\text{beat}} & T_{\text{max}} \end{pmatrix}$, where $f_{\text{beat}}$ is in hertz and $T_{\text{max}}$ is in seconds. Provide only the analytic expressions (no numerical evaluation and no units in the final answer).

### Solution

Define the blade-pass frequencies of the two fans in hertz (cycles per second). A rotational speed of $N$ rpm equals $N/60$ revolutions per second. The blade-pass frequency is the number of pressure pulses per second caused by blades passing a fixed point, which equals the number of blades times the rotational frequency.

Therefore,
$$
f_{1} = B_{1}\left(\frac{N}{60}\right) = \frac{B_{1}N}{60}, \quad
f_{2} = B_{2}\left(\frac{N+\Delta N}{60}\right) = \frac{B_{2}(N+\Delta N)}{60}.
$$

Model the two tones arriving at the listener as sinusoidal pressure signals of equal amplitude $A$:
$$
s_{1}(t) = A \cos\!\left(2\pi f_{1} t\right), \quad
s_{2}(t) = A \cos\!\left(2\pi f_{2} t\right).
$$
By linear superposition, the total signal is
$$
s(t) = s_{1}(t) + s_{2}(t) = A \cos\!\left(2\pi f_{1} t\right) + A \cos\!\left(2\pi f_{2} t\right).
$$
Use the trigonometric sum-to-product identity $\cos x + \cos y = 2 \cos\!\left(\frac{x-y}{2}\right)\cos\!\left(\frac{x+y}{2}\right)$ with $x=2\pi f_{1} t$ and $y=2\pi f_{2} t$ to obtain
$$
s(t) = 2A \cos\!\left(\pi (f_{2}-f_{1}) t\right)\cos\!\left(\pi (f_{2}+f_{1}) t\right).
$$
This shows a rapidly oscillating carrier at approximately $\frac{f_{1}+f_{2}}{2}$ with a slowly varying amplitude factor $\cos\!\left(\pi (f_{2}-f_{1}) t\right)$. The perceived loudness is related to the acoustic intensity, which is proportional to the square of the pressure signal. Thus, consider
$$
I(t) \propto s^{2}(t) = 4A^{2} \cos^{2}\!\left(\pi (f_{2}-f_{1}) t\right)\cos^{2}\!\left(\pi (f_{2}+f_{1}) t\right).
$$
The factor $\cos^{2}\!\left(\pi (f_{2}+f_{1}) t\right)$ oscillates rapidly and averages to $\frac{1}{2}$ over times long compared to the carrier period, leaving the slow modulation governed by
$$
I_{\text{env}}(t) \propto \cos^{2}\!\left(\pi \Delta f\, t\right), \quad \text{where } \Delta f \equiv \left|f_{2}-f_{1}\right|.
$$
Using the identity $\cos^{2}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1+\cos(2\theta)\right)$, the intensity envelope varies at angular frequency $2\pi \Delta f$, so the beat frequency of loudness is
$$
f_{\text{beat}} = \Delta f = \left|f_{2}-f_{1}\right|.
$$
The maxima of $I_{\text{env}}(t)$ occur whenever $\cos^{2}\!\left(\pi \Delta f\, t\right)$ reaches its maximum value, which happens at intervals of $\Delta t$ satisfying $\pi \Delta f\, \Delta t = \pi$, hence
$$
T_{\text{max}} = \frac{1}{\Delta f} = \frac{1}{\left|f_{2}-f_{1}\right|}.
$$

It remains to express $\Delta f$ in terms of $B_{1}$, $B_{2}$, $N$, and $\Delta N$. Substituting the expressions for $f_{1}$ and $f_{2}$,
$$
\Delta f = \left| \frac{B_{2}(N+\Delta N)}{60} - \frac{B_{1}N}{60} \right|
= \frac{1}{60}\left| B_{2}N + B_{2}\Delta N - B_{1}N \right|
= \frac{1}{60}\left| N(B_{2}-B_{1}) + B_{2}\Delta N \right|.
$$
Therefore,
$$
f_{\text{beat}} = \frac{1}{60}\left| N(B_{2}-B_{1}) + B_{2}\Delta N \right|, \quad
T_{\text{max}} = \frac{60}{\left| N(B_{2}-B_{1}) + B_{2}\Delta N \right|}.
$$

These expressions provide the beat frequency in hertz and the time between successive loudness maxima in seconds, as requested.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\begin{pmatrix}\frac{1}{60}\left|N(B_{2}-B_{1})+B_{2}\Delta N\right| & \frac{60}{\left|N(B_{2}-B_{1})+B_{2}\Delta N\right|}\end{pmatrix}}$$

Derive an expression for the number of floors $N$ the elevator descended, in terms of $\Delta P$, $\rho_{\text{air}}$, $g$, and $s$. Provide your final answer as a single analytic expression for $N$.

### Solution

We model the air as a static fluid of constant density. Introduce a vertical coordinate $z$ that increases upward. In a static fluid with constant density $\rho_{\text{air}}$ in a uniform gravitational field of magnitude $g$, the hydrostatic equilibrium relation is
$$\frac{dP}{dz}=-\rho_{\text{air}}\,g.$$
Integrating this relation between the initial height $z_{i}$ and the final height $z_{f}$ of the elevator gives
$$\int_{P(z_{i})}^{P(z_{f})} dP=\int_{z_{i}}^{z_{f}} -\rho_{\text{air}}\,g\,dz,$$

$$\Delta P=\rho_{\text{air}}\,g\,N\,s.$$
Solving for $N$ gives the desired expression
$$N=\frac{\Delta P}{\rho_{\text{air}}\,g\,s}.$$
This expresses the number of floors descended in terms of the measured pressure increase $\Delta P$, the air density $\rho_{\text{air}}$, gravitational acceleration $g$, and the floor spacing $s$.

$$\boxed{\frac{\Delta P}{\rho_{\text{air}} g s}}$$

Derive expressions for the instantaneous current $i(t)$ through the heater, the root-mean-square (RMS) current $I_{\text{rms}}$, and the average power dissipated in the heater $P_{\text{avg}}$, all in terms of $V_p$, $R$, and $f$. ### Solution

We model the heater as a pure resistor of resistance $R$, driven by a sinusoidal voltage $v(t)=V_p\sin(2\pi f t)$.

First, we determine the instantaneous current. For a purely resistive load, Ohm’s law holds at every instant: $v(t)=i(t)R$. Therefore,
$$
i(t)=\frac{v(t)}{R}=\frac{V_p}{R}\sin(2\pi f t).
$$

Next, we determine the RMS current $I_{\text{rms}}$. The RMS value of a time-dependent quantity $x(t)$ over one period $T=\frac{1}{f}$ is defined by
$$
x_{\text{rms}}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}x^{2}(t)\,dt}.
$$
We first compute the RMS voltage:
$$
V_{\text{rms}}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}\left[V_p\sin(2\pi f t)\right]^{2}dt}
=\sqrt{V_p^{2}\cdot\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}\sin^{2}(2\pi f t)\,dt}.
$$
Over any full period, the average of $\sin^{2}$ is $\frac{1}{2}$, so
$$
V_{\text{rms}}=\sqrt{V_p^{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}}=\frac{V_p}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$
For a resistor, $I_{\text{rms}}=\frac{V_{\text{rms}}}{R}$, hence
$$
I_{\text{rms}}=\frac{V_p}{\sqrt{2}\,R}.
$$

Now, we find the average power dissipated in the resistor. The instantaneous power is $p(t)=v(t)i(t)$. The average power over one period is
$$
P_{\text{avg}}=\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}p(t)\,dt=\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}v(t)i(t)\,dt.
$$
Using $i(t)=\frac{v(t)}{R}$, we get
$$
P_{\text{avg}}=\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}\frac{v^{2}(t)}{R}\,dt=\frac{1}{R}\cdot\frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T}\left[V_p\sin(2\pi f t)\right]^{2}dt.

$$
P_{\text{avg}}=\frac{1}{R}\cdot V_p^{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}=\frac{V_p^{2}}{2R}.
$$
This is also consistent with $P_{\text{avg}}=I_{\text{rms}}^{2}R=\left(\frac{V_p}{\sqrt{2}R}\right)^{2}R=\frac{V_p^{2}}{2R}$.

Finally, we determine the new average power when the peak voltage is reduced by 10%. The new peak voltage is $V_p' = 0.9V_p$. Substituting into the average power expression,
$$
P_{\text{avg,new}}=\frac{(V_p')^{2}}{2R}=\frac{(0.9V_p)^{2}}{2R}=\frac{0.81\,V_p^{2}}{2R}=\frac{81\,V_p^{2}}{200\,R}.
$$

Collecting the required expressions in the specified order, we have:
- $i(t)=\frac{V_p}{R}\sin(2\pi f t)$,
- $I_{\text{rms}}=\frac{V_p}{\sqrt{2}\,R}$,
- $P_{\text{avg}}=\frac{V_p^{2}}{2R}$,
- $P_{\text{avg,new}}=\frac{81\,V_p^{2}}{200\,R}$.

We present these as a single row matrix as required.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\begin{pmatrix}\frac{V_p}{R}\sin(2\pi f t)&\frac{V_p}{\sqrt{2}\,R}&\frac{V_p^{2}}{2R}&\frac{81\,V_p^{2}}{200\,R}\end{pmatrix}}$$

**Topic**: circular motion of charges in magnetic fields (0)

**Thumbnail**: In a desktop inkjet printer, charged ink droplets of charge $q$, mass $m$, and speed $v$ pass through a uniform magnetic field region of length $L$ produced by a nearby stepper motor; derive an expression for the maximum allowable field $B_{\max}$ such that the lateral deflection of a droplet’s circular path is less than one pixel pitch $p$ at the exit (model the path as an arc with $r=mv/(|q|B)$ and use the sagitta of a circle).

### Problem

An inkjet print head ejects charged ink droplets toward paper. Each droplet has charge magnitude $\left|q\right|$, mass $m$, and enters the printing zone with speed $v$ along a straight “intended print axis.” A nearby stepper motor produces a uniform magnetic field of magnitude $B$ that is exactly perpendicular to the initial droplet velocity within a finite region of space. Model this magnetic region as a slab whose upstream boundary is the plane $x=0$ and whose downstream boundary is the plane $x=L$, where $x$ measures distance along the intended print axis. Outside this slab, the magnetic field is zero. Neglect all forces other than magnetic forces while the droplet is within the slab.

Define the lateral deflection at the exit as the magnitude of the droplet’s displacement perpendicular to the intended print axis, measured at the downstream boundary $x=L$, relative to the undeflected straight-line path. To avoid visible artifacts, the printer requires that this lateral deflection be strictly less than the pixel pitch $p$.

### Solution

We model the motion only within the magnetic slab, since outside it the magnetic field is zero and the droplet travels in a straight line. Inside the slab, the velocity is perpendicular to the magnetic field, so the magnetic force has constant magnitude and is always perpendicular to the instantaneous velocity. This produces uniform circular motion.

Let the intended print axis be the $x$-axis, with the droplet entering the slab at $x=0$ with initial position at the origin and initial velocity along $+x$. Let the lateral direction be $y$. The magnetic field is uniform and perpendicular to the $x$-direction, so the droplet’s trajectory within the slab is a circular arc of constant radius. The radius of curvature follows from equating the magnetic force magnitude to the centripetal force:
$$\left|q\right| v B=\frac{m v^{2}}{r}\quad\Rightarrow\quad r=\frac{m v}{\left|q\right| B}.$$

Choose coordinates so that the center of the circular path is at $(0,r)$; this makes the initial point at $(0,0)$ and the initial tangent along $+x$, consistent with the initial conditions. A standard parametrization of the circular arc then gives the coordinates along the path in terms of the deflection angle $\theta$ from the start:
$$x(\theta)=r\sin\theta,\qquad y(\theta)=r\left(1-\cos\theta\right),$$
since at $\theta=0$ we have $(x,y)=(0,0)$ and the tangent is along $+x$.

The droplet exits the magnetic slab when it reaches the downstream boundary plane $x=L$. The exit condition is therefore
$$x(\theta_{\text{exit}})=L\quad\Rightarrow\quad L=r\sin\theta_{\text{exit}}.$$
The lateral deflection at exit, measured relative to the straight-line path (which would have $y=0$ at $x=L$), is
$$y_{\text{exit}}=y(\theta_{\text{exit}})=r\left(1-\cos\theta_{\text{exit}}\right).$$

To stay within the print tolerance, we require $y_{\text{exit}}<p$. The largest permissible magnetic field corresponds to the limiting case $y_{\text{exit}}=p$, since increasing $B$ decreases $r$ and increases the deflection. Imposing $y_{\text{exit}}=p$ and using the exit condition to eliminate $\theta_{\text{exit}}$ yields
$$p=r\left(1-\cos\theta_{\text{exit}}\right),\qquad \sin\theta_{\text{exit}}=\frac{L}{r}.$$
Using $\cos\theta_{\text{exit}}=\sqrt{1-\sin^{2}\theta_{\text{exit}}}$ (the acute angle applies since the path curves gently forward), we have
$$p=r\left(1-\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{L}{r}\right)^{2}}\right).$$
Solve this equation for $r$. Divide both sides by $r$ and isolate the square root:
$$\frac{p}{r}=1-\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{L}{r}\right)^{2}}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \sqrt{1-\left(\frac{L}{r}\right)^{2}}=1-\frac{p}{r}.$$
Square both sides:
$$1-\left(\frac{L}{r}\right)^{2}=1-\frac{2p}{r}+\left(\frac{p}{r}\right)^{2}.$$
Rearrange and multiply through by $r^{2}$:
$$\left(\frac{L}{r}\right)^{2}=\frac{2p}{r}-\left(\frac{p}{r}\right)^{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad L^{2}=2pr-p^{2}.$$
Thus,
$$2pr=L^{2}+p^{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad r=\frac{L^{2}+p^{2}}{2p}.$$

The maximum allowable magnetic field is then found by substituting this limiting radius into the radius–field relation:
$$B_{\max}=\frac{m v}{\left|q\right| r}=\frac{m v}{\left|q\right|}\cdot\frac{2p}{L^{2}+p^{2}}=\frac{2 m v p}{\left|q\right|\left(L^{2}+p^{2}\right)}.$$

This expression ensures that at $x=L$ the lateral deflection equals $p$; any larger $B$ would produce a larger deflection, violating the requirement, while any smaller $B$ would satisfy the strict inequality.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{2 m v p}{\left|q\right|\left(L^{2}+p^{2}\right)}}$$

$$6pt]
0, & \frac{1}{4f}\le t\le \frac{3}{4f}.
\end{cases}$
This is the required piecewise analytic expression in terms of the given parameters, valid over one full period.

The door opening is a vertical rectangle of width $a$ (horizontal opening), much taller than it is wide, and we idealize it as a uniform, thin aperture of width $a$ in a rigid wall. A friend stands a distance $L$ straight down the hallway from the doorway, listening to the sound field that spreads into the hall. Take the speed of sound to be $v$, so the wavelength of the tone is $\lambda=v/f$, and assume $a>\lambda$ so that at least the first off-axis minima exist. Neglect all reflections and absorption by the hallway and treat the floor as a distant observation plane at distance $L$ from the doorway.

Define the angle $\theta$ as the angle, measured in radians, between the hallway centerline (perpendicular to the doorway and bisecting the opening) and a line from the center of the doorway to a point on the floor where the sound intensity is measured. Let $y$ denote the corresponding lateral displacement on the floor from the centerline at distance $L$, with $y>0$ to the friend’s right and $y<0$ to the friend’s left.

Determine analytic expressions for the angles $\theta$ of the first minima (on both sides of the centerline) and their corresponding lateral positions $y$ on the floor at distance $L$. ### Solution

We model the doorway as a single narrow slit of width $a$ that radiates sound uniformly across the opening. In the far field (observation plane at distance $L$ large compared to $a$), the angular distribution of intensity due to a single slit is governed by interference among contributions from all points across the aperture. A standard way to determine the angular locations of minima is to compute the path difference between contributions from different parts of the slit.

Consider two rays emerging from the edges of the slit, separated by distance $a$ across the aperture. At a point on the observation plane corresponding to angle $\theta$ from the centerline, the geometric path difference between these edge rays is $a\sin\theta$. If this path difference equals an integer multiple of the wavelength, the aperture can be divided into pairs of points separated by $a/2$ whose contributions cancel pairwise due to a phase difference of $\pi$ for the first minimum, $3\pi$ for the second, and so on. The condition for destructive interference (minima) is therefore
$$
a\sin\theta = m\lambda,\quad m=\pm1,\pm2,\dots
$$
For the first minima on either side of the central maximum, we take $m=\pm1$. Thus,
$$
\sin\theta_{\pm} = \pm\frac{\lambda}{a} = \pm\frac{v}{a f}.
$$
Solving for the angles and taking principal values,
$$
\theta_{+} = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right),\quad \theta_{-} = -\arcsin\!\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right).
$$
These are in radians, as required, provided $a>\lambda$ so that $\left|\frac{v}{a f}\right|<1$.

Next, we relate the angles to the lateral positions on the floor at distance $L$. The geometry gives $y = L\tan\theta$. Therefore, for the first minima,
$$
y_{+} = L\tan\!\left(\theta_{+}\right),\quad y_{-} = L\tan\!\left(\theta_{-}\right).
$$
Using $\tan(\arcsin x) = \frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}$ for $\left|x\right|<1$, let $x=\frac{v}{a f}$. Then
$$
y_{+} = L\,\frac{\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}}
= \frac{L\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}},\quad
y_{-} = -\,\frac{L\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}}.
$$
The separation between the two first minima on the floor is
$$
\Delta y = y_{+} - y_{-} = 2\,\frac{L\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}}.
$$
If the distance $L$ is doubled to $2L$ while $a$, $f$, and $v$ are held fixed, the angles $\theta_{\pm}$ remain unchanged because they depend only on $\lambda/a = v/(a f)$. The lateral positions scale linearly with $L$, so the new separation becomes
$$
\Delta y_{\text{new}} = 2\,\frac{(2L)\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}} = 2\,\Delta y.
$$
Therefore, the multiplicative factor $R$ by which the separation changes when $L$ is doubled is $R=2$.

Collecting the required ordered results,
$$
\theta_{+} = \arcsin\!\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right),\quad
\theta_{-} = -\arcsin\!\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right),\quad
y_{+} = \frac{L\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}},\quad
y_{-} = -\frac{L\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}},\quad
R=2.
$$
These satisfy the specified units: angles in radians and positions in meters, with $R$ dimensionless.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\begin{pmatrix}
\arcsin\!\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right) & -\arcsin\!\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right) & \dfrac{L\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}} & -\dfrac{L\,\frac{v}{a f}}{\sqrt{1-\left(\frac{v}{a f}\right)^{2}}} & 2
\end{pmatrix}}$$

$$
\dot{Q}_{\text{cond}}=K\left(T_{h}-T_{c}\right).

Q_{\text{open}}=m_{\text{ex}} c_{p}\left(T_{h}-T_{c}\right)=\alpha\rho V c_{p}\left(T_{h}-T_{c}\right).

$$
E_{\text{open,hr}}=N\,\alpha\rho V c_{p}\left(T_{h}-T_{c}\right).

Factoring out the common temperature difference yields a compact form:
$$
\mathrm{COP}_{\min}=\frac{T_{h}-T_{c}}{P}\left(K+\frac{N\,\alpha\rho V c_{p}}{3600}\right).
$$
This expression is in terms of the specified variables only and represents the minimum required coefficient of performance to meet the average thermal load under the given operating pattern and assumptions.

$$\boxed{\frac{T_{h}-T_{c}}{P}\left(K+\frac{N\,\alpha\,\rho\,V\,c_{p}}{3600}\right)}$$

Second, torque equilibrium about the center of mass (at $x=0$) eliminates the torque from the weight, since its line of action passes through the pivot. Choose counterclockwise torques as positive. $$

$$

$$

\quad \Rightarrow \quad
N_{2}\left(1+\frac{d_{2}}{d_{1}}\right)=mg
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
N_{2}=\frac{mg}{1+\frac{d_{2}}{d_{1}}}
=\frac{mg\,d_{1}}{d_{1}+d_{2}}.
$$
Then
$$
N_{1}=\frac{d_{2}}{d_{1}}\,N_{2}
=\frac{d_{2}}{d_{1}}\cdot \frac{mg\,d_{1}}{d_{1}+d_{2}}
=\frac{mg\,d_{2}}{d_{1}+d_{2}}.
$$

Thus the ordered pair $(N_{1},N_{2})$ is
$$
\left(\frac{mg\,d_{2}}{d_{1}+d_{2}},\; \frac{mg\,d_{1}}{d_{1}+d_{2}}\right).
$$

$$\boxed{\begin{pmatrix}\frac{m g d_{2}}{d_{1}+d_{2}} & \frac{m g d_{1}}{d_{1}+d_{2}}\end{pmatrix}}$$

**Topic**: time constants in circuits with capacitors (1)

**Thumbnail**: A balcony rain alarm uses two exposed metal strips that become conductive when wet, effectively placing a resistance $R_w$ in parallel with a resistor $R$ that charges a capacitor $C$ to delay a chirp until the capacitor reaches a threshold fraction $f$ of the supply: derive expressions for the wet time constant $\tau_{\text{wet}}$ and for the ratio of wet-to-dry threshold-crossing times $t_{\text{wet}}/t_{\text{dry}}$ in terms of $R$, $R_w$, $C$, and $f$.

A balcony rain alarm is powered by a stable direct-current supply of voltage $V_{s}$ and uses a timing capacitor $C$ to delay a chirp tone until the capacitor voltage reaches a fixed threshold defined as a fraction $f$ of the supply, with $0<f<1$. The capacitor is initially uncharged at the moment the system powers up: $V_{C}(0)=0$. When the metal sensing strips are dry, they are effectively an open circuit, so the capacitor charges only through a resistor $R$ connected to the supply. When the strips are wet, a conductive film forms across them that can be modeled as a resistance $R_{w}$ placed in parallel with $R$, altering the charge profile of the same capacitor $C$. Assume $R$, $R_{w}$, and $C$ are constant, ideal components, the supply is ideal and steps to $V_{s}$ at $t=0$, and all other leakages and parasitics are negligible. Define $t_{\text{dry}}$ and $t_{\text{wet}}$ as the times it takes for $V_{C}(t)$ to first reach $fV_{s}$ in the dry and wet cases, respectively.

1) The wet time constant $\tau_{\text{wet}}$ of the charging network.
2) The ratio of wet-to-dry threshold-crossing times $t_{\text{wet}}/t_{\text{dry}}$.

Express your final result as a single row matrix containing $\tau_{\text{wet}}$ and $t_{\text{wet}}/t_{\text{dry}}$ in that order, using only the symbols $R$, $R_{w}$, $C$, and $f$.

### Solution

We model the charge of the capacitor from an ideal source through a Thevenin resistance seen by the capacitor node. In the dry case, only $R$ is present between $V_{s}$ and the node, so the effective resistance is $R$. In the wet case, the metal strips introduce a parallel conduction path with resistance $R_{w}$ in parallel with $R$. The equivalent resistance between the source and the node is thus
$$
R_{\text{eq,wet}} = R \parallel R_{w} = \frac{R R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}.
$$
The time constant of a first-order $RC$ charging network is $\tau = R_{\text{eq}} C$. Therefore, the wet time constant is
$$
\tau_{\text{wet}} = R_{\text{eq,wet}} C = \frac{R R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}\,C.
$$

Next, we determine the threshold-crossing time. The capacitor charging differential equation for an ideal source $V_{s}$ through resistance $R_{\text{eq}}$ into capacitance $C$ is obtained from the current relation:
$$
i_{R} = \frac{V_{s}-V_{C}(t)}{R_{\text{eq}}}, \quad i_{C} = C\,\frac{dV_{C}}{dt},
$$
and by Kirchhoff’s current law at the node, $i_{R} = i_{C}$:
$$
C\,\frac{dV_{C}}{dt} = \frac{V_{s}-V_{C}(t)}{R_{\text{eq}}}.
$$
Rewriting, we have
$$
\frac{dV_{C}}{dt} = \frac{V_{s}-V_{C}(t)}{R_{\text{eq}} C}.
$$
This linear first-order differential equation with initial condition $V_{C}(0)=0$ has the standard solution
$$
V_{C}(t) = V_{s}\left(1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{t}{R_{\text{eq}} C}\right)\right).
$$
The threshold-crossing time $t_{\text{th}}(R_{\text{eq}})$ satisfies $V_{C}(t_{\text{th}}) = f V_{s}$, so
$$
f V_{s} = V_{s}\left(1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{t_{\text{th}}}{R_{\text{eq}} C}\right)\right).
$$
Divide both sides by $V_{s}$ and solve for $t_{\text{th}}$:
$$
f = 1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{t_{\text{th}}}{R_{\text{eq}} C}\right)
\;\Rightarrow\;
\exp\!\left(-\frac{t_{\text{th}}}{R_{\text{eq}} C}\right) = 1 - f
\;\Rightarrow\;
t_{\text{th}} = - R_{\text{eq}} C \,\ln(1 - f).
$$
Therefore,
- Dry case: $R_{\text{eq,dry}} = R$, so $t_{\text{dry}} = - R C \,\ln(1 - f)$.
- Wet case: $R_{\text{eq,wet}} = \dfrac{R R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}$, so $t_{\text{wet}} = - \dfrac{R R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}\, C \,\ln(1 - f)$.

The ratio of wet-to-dry threshold-crossing times is
$$
\frac{t_{\text{wet}}}{t_{\text{dry}}}
= \frac{\left(- \dfrac{R R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}\, C \,\ln(1 - f)\right)}{\left(- R C \,\ln(1 - f)\right)}
= \frac{\dfrac{R R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}}{R}
= \frac{R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}.
$$

Collecting the requested expressions, we have
$$
\tau_{\text{wet}} = \frac{R R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}\,C,
\quad
\frac{t_{\text{wet}}}{t_{\text{dry}}} = \frac{R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}.
$$
These are expressed solely in terms of $R$, $R_{w}$, $C$, and $f$ (with the ratio independent of $C$ and $f$).

### Answer

$$\boxed{\begin{pmatrix}\frac{R R_{w} C}{R+R_{w}} & \frac{R_{w}}{R+R_{w}}\end{pmatrix}}$$

**Topic**: Faraday law and induced emf (1)

**Thumbnail**: During a stage setup, a foldable square aluminum truss with side length $s(t)=s_0+vt$ is being opened at constant rate between the poles of a large electromagnet whose field decreases as $B(t)=B_0-\beta t$, with the truss’s normal aligned with the field, so derive the instantaneous induced emf $\mathcal{E}(t)$ around the truss accounting for both the changing area and the changing field.

The loop’s normal remains aligned with the field direction at all times, and the field is uniform over the entire loop area. Assume the loop remains electrically continuous and that any back-reaction on the field is negligible.

Derive an explicit expression for the magnitude of the instantaneous induced electromotive force (emf) $\mathcal{E}(t)$ around the loop as a function of $t$. Provide your final answer as a single closed-form analytic expression (no numerical substitutions).

### Solution

We model the truss as a single square conducting loop of side length $s(t)$ in a spatially uniform, time-varying magnetic field $B(t)$ that is perpendicular to the loop. The magnetic flux through the loop is given by $\Phi(t)=B(t)A(t)\cos\theta$, where $A(t)$ is the loop area and $\theta$ is the angle between the field and the loop’s normal. Since the normal is aligned with the field at all times, $\theta=0$ and $\cos\theta=1$, so $\Phi(t)=B(t)A(t)$.

The area of the square loop is $A(t)=[s(t)]^{2}=(s_{0}+v t)^{2}$. The field is $B(t)=B_{0}-\beta t$. Faraday’s law of electromagnetic induction for a closed conducting loop states that the induced electromotive force (emf) is the magnitude of the negative time rate of change of the magnetic flux, i.e., $\mathcal{E}(t)=\left|-\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\right|=\left|\frac{d}{dt}\left(B(t)A(t)\right)\right|$.

Using the product rule for differentiation,
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(B(t)A(t)\right)=A(t)\frac{dB}{dt}+B(t)\frac{dA}{dt}.
$$
Compute the needed derivatives:
- $\frac{dB}{dt}=-\beta$,
- $\frac{dA}{dt}=\frac{d}{dt}\left[(s_{0}+v t)^{2}\right]=2(s_{0}+v t)\cdot v$.

Substitute these and the expressions for $A(t)$ and $B(t)$:
$$
\frac{d\Phi}{dt}=(s_{0}+v t)^{2}(-\beta)+(B_{0}-\beta t)\left[2 v (s_{0}+v t)\right].
$$
Factor out $(s_{0}+v t)$:
$$
\frac{d\Phi}{dt}=(s_{0}+v t)\left[-\beta (s_{0}+v t)+2 v (B_{0}-\beta t)\right].
$$
Expand the bracket to simplify:
$$
-\beta (s_{0}+v t)+2 v (B_{0}-\beta t)=-\beta s_{0}-\beta v t+2 v B_{0}-2 v \beta t
=2 v B_{0}-\beta s_{0}-3 v \beta t.
$$
Therefore,
$$
\frac{d\Phi}{dt}=(s_{0}+v t)\left(2 v B_{0}-\beta s_{0}-3 v \beta t\right).
$$
Taking the magnitude for the induced emf,
$$
\mathcal{E}(t)=\left|\frac{d\Phi}{dt}\right|=\left|(s_{0}+v t)\left(2 v B_{0}-\beta s_{0}-3 v \beta t\right)\right|.
$$
This expression accounts for both the time dependence of the area and the time dependence of the magnetic field.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\left|(s_{0}+v t)\left(2 v B_{0}-\beta s_{0}-3 v \beta t\right)\right|}$$

By Archimedes’ principle, for a small additional immersion depth x (positive downward), the additional displaced volume is Sx, resulting in an additional buoyant force of magnitude ρg(Sx). This force is upward if x is downward and vice versa, so the linear restoring force is F = −ρgS x. This identifies an effective stiffness k = ρgS, and the equation of motion for vertical displacement x is that of simple harmonic motion (SHM): m x'' + k x = 0, where m is the buoy’s mass. The angular frequency is ω = √(k/m), but we do not need its explicit value to relate amplitude and energy.

For SHM, the total mechanical energy E is constant (in the absence of dissipation) and is the sum of kinetic and potential energies:
E = (1/2) m v^{2} + (1/2) k x^{2}.
At a turning point, the speed v = 0 and the displacement reaches its maximum magnitude A, so the total energy equals the elastic (hydrostatic) potential energy at that point:
E = (1/2) k A^{2}.
Substituting k = ρgS gives the amplitude–energy relation:
A = √(2E/k) = √(2E/(ρgS)).

Now consider two states: an initial state with energy E₀ and amplitude A₀, and a later state with energy E = 0.64E₀ and amplitude A. Using the same relation for both,
A₀ = √(2E₀/(ρgS)), and A = √(2E/(ρgS)).
Taking the ratio,
A/A₀ = √(E/E₀) = √(0.64) = 0.8.

Therefore, the amplitude scales with the square root of energy, and a reduction of energy to 0.64E₀ reduces the amplitude by a factor of 0.8.

$$\boxed{0.8}$$

and the unpolarized part has intensity
$$

We model the cart and LIDAR as a single body of mass $m$ that emits a steady stream of photons in the $+x$ direction. Each photon carries momentum and energy, and the emitted beam therefore carries away momentum per unit time, exerting a reaction force on the cart in the $-x$ direction.

$$

$$

$$

Thus the recoil acceleration is constant and negative (opposite the beam direction), and the displacement is a quadratic function of time with the same sign.

$$
\tan\phi = \frac{\left|v_{x}\right|}{\left|v_{y}\right|}.
$$

Applying this to $\vec{v}_{\text{rain rel car}} = (-v_{\text{car}}, -v_{r})$, we have $\left|v_{x}\right| = v_{\text{car}}$ and $\left|v_{y}\right| = v_{r}$, yielding
$$
\tan\phi = \frac{v_{\text{car}}}{v_{r}}.
$$

This expression is dimensionless and depends only on the ratio of the car’s horizontal speed to the rain’s downward speed.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{v_{\text{car}}}{v_{r}}}$$

## Problem 56

**Topic**: projectile motion components and trajectories (0)

**Thumbnail**: At a rooftop cafe, a person at height $H$ fires a foam dart at speed $v$ at angle $\theta$ toward a billboard a horizontal distance $D$ away; derive an expression for the impact height on the billboard, $y(D)$, in terms of $H$, $v$, $\theta$, $D$, and $g$.

### Problem

At a rooftop cafe, a person launches a foam dart from a point that is at a vertical height H above street level. The dart is launched with initial speed v at an angle θ above the horizontal toward a large vertical billboard located a horizontal distance D away, measured along a straight, level street. Assume the only acceleration is due to gravity, which has constant magnitude g acting downward, and ignore air resistance. Let the vertical coordinate y be measured upward from street level directly beneath the launcher, and let the horizontal coordinate x be measured from the same point along the street toward the billboard. The dart impacts the billboard at the moment it reaches horizontal position x = D. Derive an expression for the impact height on the billboard, y(D), in terms of H, v, θ, D, and g. Assume θ is measured in radians. Express your final result as a single analytic expression in terms of the given symbols only; do not substitute numerical values or include units.

### Solution

We model the dart’s flight as two-dimensional motion with constant horizontal velocity and constant vertical acceleration due to gravity. We set up a coordinate system with origin at street level directly below the launch point. The initial position is therefore x(0) = 0 and y(0) = H. The initial velocity components are determined by the launch speed v and angle θ above the horizontal: the horizontal component is v_{x0} = v\cos(\theta) and the vertical component is v_{y0} = v\sin(\theta).

Under constant gravitational acceleration g acting downward, the kinematic equations for the components are:
x(t) = v\cos(\theta)\,t,
y(t) = H + v\sin(\theta)\,t - \frac{1}{2} g t^{2}.

The dart reaches the billboard plane when the horizontal position equals x = D. We solve x(t) = D for the time of flight to that plane:
D = v\cos(\theta)\,t \implies t = \frac{D}{v\cos(\theta)}.

Substitute this time into the vertical position function to obtain the impact height y(D):
y(D) = H + v\sin(\theta)\left(\frac{D}{v\cos(\theta)}\right) - \frac{1}{2} g \left(\frac{D}{v\cos(\theta)}\right)^{2}.

Simplify term by term. The middle term simplifies using v\sin(\theta)/(v\cos(\theta)) = \tan(\theta):
v\sin(\theta)\left(\frac{D}{v\cos(\theta)}\right) = D\tan(\theta).

The last term becomes:
\frac{1}{2} g \left(\frac{D^{2}}{v^{2}\cos^{2}(\theta)}\right) = \frac{g D^{2}}{2 v^{2} \cos^{2}(\theta)}.

Therefore, the expression for the impact height on the billboard is:
y(D) = H + D\tan(\theta) - \frac{g D^{2}}{2 v^{2} \cos^{2}(\theta)}.

This expression is written purely in terms of the given symbols H, v, θ, D, and g, as required.

### Answer

$$\boxed{H + D\tan(\theta) - \frac{g D^{2}}{2 v^{2}\cos^{2}(\theta)}}$$

## Problem 57

**Topic**: resistivity and temperature dependence (0)

**Thumbnail**: A winter pop-up vendor powers a resistive space heater of constant resistance $R_h$ through a long copper extension cord whose resistance varies with ambient temperature as $R_c(T)=R_{c0}(1+\alpha\,\Delta T)$; assuming a constant supply voltage $V$, derive an expression for the fractional change in power actually dissipated by the heater, $P_h(T)/P_h(0)-1$, in terms of $R_h$, $R_{c0}$, $\alpha$, and $\Delta T$.

### Problem

In a winter street market, a vendor runs a resistive space heater of constant electrical resistance $R_h$ from a constant-voltage source of potential difference $V$ through a long copper extension cord. The extension cord’s electrical resistance depends on the ambient temperature $T$ according to a linear model
$$R_c(T)=R_{c0}\left(1+\alpha\,\Delta T\right),$$
where $R_{c0}$ is the cord’s resistance at a reference ambient temperature, $\alpha$ is the temperature coefficient of resistance, and $\Delta T=T-T_{\text{ref}}$ is the ambient temperature change relative to that reference. The heater’s resistance $R_h$ is unaffected by ambient temperature over the range considered. The heater and cord are connected in series.

Let $P_h(T)$ be the electrical power actually dissipated in the heater at ambient temperature $T$. Let $P_h(0)$ denote the heater power at the reference temperature (i.e., when $\Delta T=0$). Derive an analytic expression for the fractional change in heater power, $P_h(T)/P_h(0)-1$, in terms of $R_h$, $R_{c0}$, $\alpha$, and $\Delta T$. Provide only the symbolic expression as your final answer.

### Solution

We model the heater and the extension cord as two series resistances, $R_h$ and $R_c(T)$, connected across a constant-voltage source $V$. For resistors in series, the same current flows through both elements, and the total series resistance is $R_{\text{tot}}(T)=R_h+R_c(T)$. By Ohm’s law, the circuit current at ambient temperature $T$ is
$$I(T)=\frac{V}{R_h+R_c(T)}.$$
The power dissipated specifically in the heater at temperature $T$ is given by the power relation $P=I^{2}R$ applied to the heater:
$$P_h(T)=I(T)^{2}R_h=\frac{V^{2}R_h}{\left(R_h+R_c(T)\right)^{2}}.$$
At the reference temperature, $\Delta T=0$, we have $R_c(0)=R_{c0}$, so the baseline heater power is
$$P_h(0)=\frac{V^{2}R_h}{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\right)^{2}}.$$
Forming the ratio gives
$$\frac{P_h(T)}{P_h(0)}=\frac{\dfrac{V^{2}R_h}{\left(R_h+R_c(T)\right)^{2}}}{\dfrac{V^{2}R_h}{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\right)^{2}}}=\frac{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\right)^{2}}{\left(R_h+R_c(T)\right)^{2}}.$$
Substituting the linear temperature dependence $R_c(T)=R_{c0}\left(1+\alpha\,\Delta T\right)$ yields
$$\frac{P_h(T)}{P_h(0)}=\frac{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\right)^{2}}{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\left(1+\alpha\,\Delta T\right)\right)^{2}}.$$
Therefore, the fractional change in heater power is
$$\frac{P_h(T)}{P_h(0)}-1=\frac{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\right)^{2}}{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\left(1+\alpha\,\Delta T\right)\right)^{2}}-1,$$
which is the required expression in terms of $R_h$, $R_{c0}$, $\alpha$, and $\Delta T$.

### Answer

$$\boxed{\frac{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\right)^{2}}{\left(R_h+R_{c0}\left(1+\alpha\,\Delta T\right)\right)^{2}}-1}$$

## Problem 58

**Topic**: escape velocity calculations (0)

**Thumbnail**: A mining drone on a roughly spherical asteroid measures its radius $R$ and average density $\rho$; neglecting rotation and outgassing, derive the minimum straight-up launch speed needed for a tossed sample to never return in terms of $G$, $\rho$, and $R$.

### Problem

A small autonomous mining drone operates on a roughly spherical asteroid. Using onboard instruments, it determines the asteroid’s radius to be a known symbol $R$ and its average mass density to be a known symbol $\rho$. Assume the asteroid can be modeled as a perfect sphere with uniform density equal to the measured average density $\rho$. Ignore any rotation of the asteroid, any atmosphere or outgassing, and any external forces other than the asteroid’s own gravity.

The drone uses a simple spring mechanism to toss a tiny sample straight upward from the asteroid’s surface and then provides no further propulsion. In terms of the universal gravitational constant $G$, the asteroid’s radius $R$, and its average density $\rho$, derive the minimum launch speed required so that the tossed sample never returns to the asteroid.

Express your answer as a single closed-form analytic expression in terms of $G$, $\rho$, and $R$ only.

### Solution

We begin by modeling the asteroid as a sphere of uniform density $\rho$ and radius $R$. The total mass $M$ of the asteroid is related to its density by the volume of a sphere:
$$
M=\rho \times \text{Volume}=\rho \left(\frac{4}{3}\pi R^{3}\right)=\frac{4}{3}\pi \rho R^{3}.
$$
Outside a spherically symmetric mass distribution, the gravitational field and potential are the same as if all the mass were concentrated at the center. Therefore, for a sample of mass $m$ at a distance $r \geq R$ from the center, the gravitational potential energy is
$$
U(r)=-\frac{G M m}{r},
$$
and by convention $U(\infty)=0$.

The minimum speed for the sample to never return corresponds to it reaching infinitely far away with zero speed. This means its mechanical energy at launch from the surface must equal the potential energy at infinity. At the surface ($r=R$), the initial mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic and potential energies:
$$
E_{\text{initial}}=\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}-\frac{G M m}{R}.
$$
At infinity, the final mechanical energy is
$$
E_{\text{final}}=0.
$$
Conservation of mechanical energy for motion under gravity gives
$$
\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}-\frac{G M m}{R}=0.
$$
Solving for $v$,
$$
\frac{1}{2} m v^{2}=\frac{G M m}{R}\quad\Rightarrow\quad v^{2}=\frac{2 G M}{R}.
$$
Substitute $M=\frac{4}{3}\pi \rho R^{3}$:
$$
v^{2}=\frac{2 G}{R}\left(\frac{4}{3}\pi \rho R^{3}\right)=\frac{8\pi G \rho}{3} R^{2}.
$$
Taking the positive square root for speed,
$$
v=R\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G \rho}{3}}.
$$
This expression depends only on $G$, $\rho$, and $R$, as required.

### Answer

$$\boxed{R\sqrt{\frac{8\pi G \rho}{3}}}$$
